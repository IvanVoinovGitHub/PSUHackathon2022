The Humanitarian Crisis

In 2022, 274 million people will need humanitarian assistance and protection.

This number is a significant increase from 235 million people a year ago, which was already the highest figure in decades. The United Nations and partner organizations aim to assist 183 million people most in need across 63 countries, which will require $41 billion.

With so many people needing humanitarian aid and with limited amount of funding, it is imperative that we make the most of the limited funding we donate. BiggerImpact has revolutionized the way aid is distributed, optimizing how every penny is spent. Not only is money going further, but it is also tackling the COVID-19 pandemic.

GLOBAL HUMANITARIAN CRISIS OVERVIEW 

COVID-19 Methodology

Originating on December 12th, 2019 in Wuhan, China, the SARS-CoV-2 virus has subsequently spread across the world, reaching a case count of over 330 million and a death count of over 5.5 million. With concern for public health in consideration, the ability to predict and suppress the spread of COVID-19 is imperative to tackling this pandemic.
``Most people infected with the virus will experience mild to moderate respiratory illness and recover without requiring special treatment. However, some will become seriously ill and require medical attention. Older people and those with underlying medical conditions like cardiovascular disease, diabetes, chronic respiratory disease, or cancer are more likely to develop serious illness. Anyone can get sick with COVID-19 and become seriously ill or die at any age." (World Health Organization) 

To tackle and mitigate the spread and damage of COVID-19, the world must unite together. The prevalence of COVID-19 in neighboring and even distant countries has lasting effects on all of us. The more the SARS-CoV-2 virus spreads, not only do more people suffer, but the more likely a new variant will arise; a new COVID-19 variant(of-concern) is very dangerous to everyone. Due to these reasons, it is in everyone's best interest to help combat the spread of COVID-19 both locally and overseas. 

Evaluating COVID-19 Risk in Countries in Distress

To assess the COVID-19 risk level for the countries that have humanitarian crises, the countries' COVID-19 daily infection rate was used to classify a risk level. The different risk level thresholds have been created by Brown University. The four categories are "Green", "Yellow", "Orange", and "Red" in increasing severity. For future reference, "1", "2", "3", and "4" may be used interchangeably. The diagram below illustrates these four classifications.

As depicted here on the left, approximately 55% of the 29 countries with humanitarian crises are considered to be in the "RED" Covid-19 risk level. Countries with humanitarian crises tend to have higher rates of poverty, worse sanitation & water access, lack of healthcare infrastructure, and a lack of access to vaccinations. With all these factors considered, the COVID-19 spread is exponentially more difficult to curb in these countries.

To evaluate a priority queue of countries that need the most help, a risk score was evaluated for each country. The higher the risk score, the more urgent it is to send humanitarian aid to that country.                                                                                                                                                        Risk Score = (Risk Level) x (Number of People In Need)                                                                                                                This formula was used to assess the urgency of aid with the following reasoning:                                                              1. A larger amount of people in need in a humanitarian crisis typically indicates that the crisis is larger and is impacting more people                                                            2. Countries with higher risk levels are more prone to the spread of COVID-19                                                                3. COVID-19 is more devastating to a larger population both in terms of spread and symptom severity
